By Moses

Known as “the cradle of mankind,” Kenya has no single culture that identifies it. There are about 40 different ethnic groups in Kenya, each with its unique culture. As a result, there is no single dish that represents all of Kenya. The staple foods in Kenya include cereals (maize, millet, sorghum and others depending on the region) eaten with various meats and vegetables. Foods that are universally eaten in Kenya are ugali, sukuma wiki and githeri.

North African food

Cuisine of the Maghreb countries, Algerian cuisine, Moroccan cuisine and Tunisian cuisine. North Africa borders the Mediterranean Sea and includes Algeria, Morocco, Libya, Tunisia, Mauritania and Egypt. This region is marked by its geographical, political, social, economic and cultural diversity. The cuisine, culinary style and art of North Africa are as diverse as its regions, peoples and history. The roots of North African cuisine can be traced back to the ancient empires of North Africa, particularly Egypt, where many of the country's dishes and culinary traditions date back to antiquity. From the seventh century, the Arabs introduced a variety of spices, such as saffron, nutmeg, cinnamon, ginger and cloves, which contributed to and influenced the culinary culture of North Africa. The Ottoman Turks brought pastries and other baked goods.

Central African food

Central Africa stretches from the Tibesti massif in northern Chad to the great rainforest at the source of the Congo Basin, the Kivu highlands and the Katanga savannahs. This region has received several culinary influences. The Swahili (a culture that evolved In the western region, especially in the Kongo Kingdom, there was a Portuguese culinary influence. An example of this is the salted fish called makayabu from the Portuguese word Bacalhau (bal-ca-ya-hu). These foods have had a great influence on local cuisine and preparation methods. Nevertheless, the culinary influence found in Central Africa is also transcontinental. Central Africa shares several dishes with other African regions because of their proximity. Ntaba (goat) or lamb) in barbecue style with chikwangue and chilli dust The basic ingredients are plantains, cassava, chikwangue and rice. Fufu, a starch-based dish, is most often made from fermented cassava roots, but also includes plantains, maize and yam. Fufu is served as a buffet with meat and/or fish, sauces, vegetables and chilli.

Kenyan food

Kenyan cuisine is very varied and differs from one region (natural resources) and especially from one ethnic group (traditions) to another. There are however two common culinary preparations: Ugali (a word of Swahili origin). It can be made only with white maize (mahindi in Swahili) or with sorghum (mtama in Swahili). Githeri (a word of Kikuyu origin), which is a mixture of maize grains and beans. This mixture may or may not be accompanied by other vegetables, depending on the tribal origin. Nshima, a maize-based dish mixed with vegetables and meat (or fish), is also sometimes eaten. The side dishes may consist of ugali (also called kwon in Luo) made of yellow or white maize with either sukuma wiki (which currently means "a dish of cabbage" but is a misnomer; in Swahili, sukuma means to grow and wiki means week. Originally, therefore, it meant "the art of preparing leftovers") or of scalded and browned alot bo or kunde leaves (in Luo or Swahili). Chapati (in Swahili) with dengu (in Swahili) or other oganda or maharagwe ('beans' in Luo or Swahili). Kachumbari ("tomato, onion and coriander salad with varying degrees of spice" in Swahili). Plantain or sweet potato. Okra (cooked in a tomato sauce or mixed with sukuma wiki or alot bo). Apoth ("slippery vegetable" in Luo), which is the leaf of a wild herb that tastes like okra. Everything can be washed down with a local beer (Tusker or Senator) or palm wine.
